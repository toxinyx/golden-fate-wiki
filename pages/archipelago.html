Heiwa 平和.

Archipelago: a collection [or group] of islands, usually small.

平和; Japanese for ‘Peace’, Heiwa is an archipelago located in the Pacific Ocean. Formed in 492 BES from a series of underwater volcanic eruptions, initially, these islands were not inhabitable by people and instead were left alone for around 87 years. After that, at some point, well-intended travellers decided to send professionals on clean-up duty with the intent of making it a suitable place to live, which they did exactly. It is a collection of four islands, albeit three are significantly smaller than one. The population of Heiwa in it’s entirety is around 135,000,000. Over time, each island has seemed to become adapted to fit a specific purpose, but none are segregated from each other — people are free to come and go for the most part.

A notable fact is that children are often taught Japanese alongside English, together. Heiwa tries to be a pinnacle of acceptance and progressiveness, albeit some of the different kingdoms make that a very difficult task to uphold. Many Japanese traditions have carried over from before the Epoch Shift and have been kept alive and well. Though as more people arrived on the Island, culture became diverse and expanded to a lot of different Asian and European countries as well.

平和; Heiwa || Main Island

The main island of Heiwa, it is the biggest by a long shot. There are a variety of kingdoms located on this island, aiming to try and live peacefully separated by their territory lines.

Vampire and Oni Territory

At the top of Heiwa, buried deep within the mountain, Vampires and Oni’s share a wide spread of land, separated into their respective kingdoms. These are largely mountainside villages and cities, with a large currently dormant volcano separating the two kingdoms. They live in relative seclusion most of the time, as the mountains are not easy terrain to travel over for typical people.

Touwara

Touwara is the City of Vampirism; surrounding the Vampire Palace for miles out, it is a town truly stuck in the past.
